Redesign K-Drama Webpage

(Case Study)



Roles: UX/UI Designer, Prototyper and Web Designer

Tools: Adobe XD, Indesign

Skills: Wireframing, Prototyping

Time: Three to Four Weeks





Problem:


The information page for Korean Dramas (K-dramas) located in wikipedia, is not user freindly and needs to be re-deisgned so users can understand and look for the content they like.

Solution (Goal):


The solution is to create a website that can display the information about k-dramas in the most 'user-friendly' way by conducting user reserach to understand the needs of the user (to re-design the website).



"How can we make the user interface more user friendly?"


Reserach:


To understand who the users are and where they come from, I analyzed some statistics and articles. From the first article, there were around 8,000 users who responded to the survey question. The article explored behaviours, interests of the audience who watched Korean dramas specifically from Netflix. In order to understand the users goals, challenges, attitudes and behaviours, I had to understand what their background is, also if they have any other hobbies and interests.

The research about the users covers their age, gender, occupation, ethnicity, geographic location, relationship status, degree level, and sexual orientation. Specific information such as how they were introduced to kdramas, what genres they like, how long they have been watching kdrama, where they watch it, and if they know how to speak or understand the language.

User Personas:


The personas were created after analyzing statistics and articles.

Lia’s persona is a student and is employed and makes up 10.1% of viewers who work and are in school. Because Lia's persona identifies as a female, the female watchers makeup around 80.2%.

Chaloem’s persona is a lawyer who is employed and makes up 44.9% of the viewers who are also employed. Chaloem’s occupation makes up 3.1% of viewers who work in the legal sector.

Heidi and 15.7% of viewers were introduced to kdramas by family members. Heidi has been watching kdramas for three to four years and make up the 9.5% viewers who have been watching for this long. Heidi and 2.2% of other users watch kdramas on DVD.



Information Architecture:


This sitemap aims to meet the requirements of a variety of users. Including those users who may not have much experience with technology. Like the user persona Heidi who rarely browses on the web. The following subheadings: early years, 1980s and the rating system are placed in the local navigation. This is because, all of these headings explain the history and include information about the ratings.

Navigation (IA):


This version of the sitemap aims to be 'concise' and 'clear'. It helps users to navigate faster, for example if users are looking for specific genres like the user persona Heidi, who likes watching both romance and historical genres. While her husband prefers the historical genre. If users want to browse Korean dramas related to those genres, users are able to click on the global navigation heading ‘genres’ and choose the local navigation heading ‘romance’ to look for dramas that are romance related. If they are looking for historical dramas, users are able to click on the local navigation heading ‘historical’.

Wireframing:


The global navigation has five specific sections that lets the user know what they are clicking on. The local navigation options are carefully selected in order to make the users understand what the content is, in a simple and concise way. Users like Chaloem, who want to find popular actors and their work, can easily go to the global navigation and click on the local navigation heading 'actors and actresses'. This will lead him to a page about successful actors that have gained popularity because of their amazing acting in dramas. The context will also go into detail of suggested Korean dramas to watch that cast those actors and actresses.

The search button is the utility navigation, which allows users to do a quick search if they are using the website for the first time and are not sure how to navigate it. For example, a user like Heidi who does not use technology often, will be able to find what they are looking for after a simple serach.

The glossary will help explain words that might sound unfamiliar to any user. Especially since there are some Korean words that do not have 'direct' translations, instead users will be able to check the glossary to recevie an in depth explantion.



Branding:






The logo uses two main colours of two shades: blue and a bit of red. These two colours are used in the flag of Korea and the ‘O’ in the logo shows an illustration of the Korean flag. These two specific shades are aesthetically pleasing to the eyes and gives a minimalistic look. The logo is more on the 'fun' side and gives a welcoming vibe.



Finalize:




Both pages are designed to have only important content and reduce extra links/images that could be distracting. As we do not want our users to think and instead let the options unfold in front of them. The typography was chosen carefully to make sure it is easy to read for users. The readability is clear and users like Heidi who do not use technology as much, will be able to read and understand everything. Users similar to Lia and Chaloem who do not have much time to read big paragraphs of information, are able to read and process everything on the screen quickly. The homepage and the history page strives to demonstrate the UX design basics (scannability, visual hierarchy, contrast, readability).
